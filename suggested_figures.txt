SECTION: 2-robot-localisation.tex
========================================================================

FIGURE 1 — Dead-reckoning drift vs. corrected trajectory
----------------------------------------------------------
What: Side-by-side comparison of a robot path under pure odometry
      (showing compounding drift) vs. a SLAM-corrected trajectory. Makes
      the "Limits of Deterministic Localisation" subsection concrete.
Where to get it:
  - Ramezani et al. (2020), "Online LiDAR-SLAM for Legged Robots",
    Figure showing odometric vs. SLAM-corrected map.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2001.10249v1
  - Alternatively, Figure 1 of Hroob et al. (2021) (vineyard SLAM
    benchmark) shows per-row drift in agricultural settings.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.05283v1

FIGURE 2 — Smith–Cheeseman covariance ellipses (compounding & merging)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
What: Diagram showing a robot pose with an uncertainty ellipse, and how
      composing two uncertain transforms grows the ellipse (compounding),
      vs. fusing two independent estimates shrinking it (merging). Directly
      illustrates the "Uncertainty Representation and Sensor Fusion"
      subsection.
Where to get it:
  - Original: Smith & Cheeseman (1987), "On the Representation and
    Estimation of Spatial Uncertainty", Int. Journal of Robotics Research.
    Not on arXiv, but widely reproduced. DOI: 10.1177/027836498700600301
  - Good reproduction in: Thrun, Burgard & Fox (2005), "Probabilistic
    Robotics", Chapter 3 (available as PDF from authors' website).

FIGURE 3 — ICP alignment iterations (point cloud registration)
--------------------------------------------------------------
What: Step-by-step visualisation of ICP converging two overlapping point
      clouds — source vs. target before alignment, mid-iteration, and
      final registered result. Supports the "Point Cloud Registration"
      subsection.
Where to get it:
  - Semantic-ICP paper (Chen et al., 2025), Fig. 1 and Fig. 3 show
    point-to-point correspondence and alignment stages.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.00972v3
  - Single-tree cloud registration (Ren et al., 2024) has clear before/
    after registration visuals with tree point clouds (arguably more
    relevant to a vineyard context).
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.13069v1

FIGURE 4 — Pose graph with loop-closure correction
---------------------------------------------------
What: Schematic of a pose graph (nodes = poses, edges = constraints)
      before and after a loop closure is detected, showing how the
      correction propagates backward through the trajectory. Core to the
      "Pose Graph Optimisation and Global Consistency" subsection.
Where to get it:
  - Denniston et al. (2022), "Loop Closure Prioritization for Efficient
    and Scalable Multi-Robot SLAM", has clear pose-graph diagrams.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2205.12402v2
  - Ghanta & Parasuraman (2025), "Policies over Poses: RL-based
    Distributed PGO for Multi-Robot SLAM", Fig. 1 shows graph structure.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.22740v1
  - Ramezani et al. (2020, above) Fig. 2 shows the factor-graph layout
    used in LiDAR-SLAM with loop closure.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2001.10249v1

SECTION: 2-vineyard-pruning.tex (Robot Localisation in Vineyard Pruning)
========================================================================

FIGURE 5 — Dormant vineyard scene with LiDAR point cloud overlay
-----------------------------------------------------------------
What: A photograph or render of a bare-cane winter vineyard alongside
      (or overlaid with) the corresponding LiDAR scan, visually showing
      the sparse point density that motivates the difficulty statement in
      the opening paragraph of the section.
Where to get it:
  - Bertoglio et al. (2023), "A Map-Free LiDAR-Based System for
    Autonomous Navigation in Vineyards", Figs. 4–6 show the in-field
    LiDAR scan data of actual vineyard rows.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2307.03080v1
  - Hroob et al. (2021), "Benchmark of Visual and 3D LiDAR SLAM in
    Vineyards", Figs. 1–2 show the simulated and real vineyard
    environments with visible sensor data.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.05283v1

FIGURE 6 — 3D skeletal reconstruction of a dormant grapevine
-------------------------------------------------------------
What: A rendered 3D point cloud or mesh skeleton of a single grapevine
      structure (showing trunk, cordons, and canes). Illustrates what the
      robot's sensors actually see and what must be registered.
Where to get it:
  - Schneider et al. (2023), "3D Skeletonization of Complex Grapevines
    for Robotic Pruning", Figs. 4–7 show dense real-world grapevine
    point clouds and their skeletons.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2307.11706v1
  - Dobbs et al. (2025), "Accurate 3D Grapevine Structure Extraction
    from High-Resolution Point Clouds", Figs. 2–4 show skeleton
    extraction on real-world scans.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.20417v1

FIGURE 7 — Pruning-point detection via semantic segmentation
------------------------------------------------------------
What: An image of a dormant vine with a neural segmentation mask
      overlaid, highlighting detected canes and candidate cut points.
      Supports the discussion of deep panoptic segmentation systems
      (Navone et al., Archie Jnr).
Where to get it:
  - Fernandes et al. (2021), "Towards Precise Pruning Points Detection
    using Semantic-Instance-Aware Plant Models", Figs. 3–5 show
    segmentation masks with detected pruning points marked.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2109.07247v1
  - Fernandes et al. (2021), "Grapevine Winter Pruning Automation…
    2D Plant Modeling", Figs. 4–6 show the earlier version of the
    pipeline from raw image to point candidates.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2106.04208v1

FIGURE 8 — Vineyard row template / LiDAR-based row localisation
---------------------------------------------------------------
What: A top-down view of a robot traversing a vineyard row with the
      LiDAR scan overlaid, showing how the row geometry is used for
      lateral and heading localisation. Relevant to the LiDAR-based
      approach of Felicetti et al. and motivates the map-building work
      in Chapter 3.
Where to get it:
  - Fei & Vougioukas (2021), "Row-Sensing Templates: A Generic 3D
    Sensor-Based Approach to Robot Localisation in Orchards", Figs. 3–6
    show point clouds and matching results in a real vineyard.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.01321v1
  - Bertoglio et al. (2023, above) Figs. 7–9 show the navigation path
    within rows with error plots.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2307.03080v1

FIGURE 9 — Autonomous robotic pruning platform in the field
-----------------------------------------------------------
What: A photograph of a robotic pruning platform operating in a
      vineyard, providing visual context for the systems discussed
      (Botterill's robot, Archie Jnr, or similar). Useful as an
      introductory or contextualising figure for the section.
Where to get it:
  - Navone et al. (2025), "Autonomous Robotic Pruning in Orchards and
    Vineyards: a Review", Fig. 1 and throughout shows multiple platform
    photographs from the literature.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.07318v1
  - Milburn et al. (2023), "Computer-Vision Based Vineyard Navigation
    for Quadruped Robots", Fig. 1 shows the HyQReal quadruped in a
    vineyard.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2301.00887v1

FIGURE 10 — SLAM benchmark results in a vineyard (trajectory comparison)
-------------------------------------------------------------------------
What: A quantitative trajectory accuracy comparison across SLAM methods
      (e.g., LIO-SAM, ORB-SLAM2, RTAB-MAP) in a vineyard environment,
      demonstrating that SLAM performance in this domain is non-trivial
      and motivating the Chapter 3 system design.
Where to get it:
  - Hroob et al. (2021), "Benchmark of Visual and 3D LiDAR SLAM Systems
    in Simulation Environment for Vineyards", Figs. 5–8 show estimated
    trajectories and error tables across methods.
    arXiv: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2107.05283v1
