# Stereo-Depth Camera Imaging  
Recent works use stereo or RGB-D cameras to capture vine geometry. For example, Klodt *et al.* (2015) developed a dense stereo reconstruction method to generate depth maps of grapevines, enabling robust background subtraction and precise 3D phenotyping of leaf area and fruit-to-leaf ratios【12†L19-L23】. Parr *et al.* (2022) benchmark multiple depth cameras (structured light, active IR stereo, ToF and LiDAR) on grape clusters; they show that active IR stereo cameras (e.g. Intel RealSense) can operate in sunlight and achieve millimeter‐scale accuracy for vine canopy mapping【2†L157-L165】. These studies demonstrate that affordable stereo-depth sensors can capture detailed 3D point clouds of vines under field conditions. 

- **Klodt et al. (2015)** – *Field phenotyping of grapevine using dense stereo reconstruction (BMC Bioinformatics)*.  Presents a stereo imaging pipeline that produces dense depth maps of vines from two RGB images. The authors apply this to segment canopy vs. background and compute 3D leaf areas and fruit‐leaf ratios, showing that stereo cameras can yield accurate vine structure without special backgrounds【12†L19-L23】.  
- **Parr et al. (2022)** – *Analysis of Depth Cameras for Proximal Sensing of Grapes (Sensors)*.  Compares five depth sensors (including Kinect V2/Azure, Intel RealSense D415, L515 LiDAR) on grape clusters. They report that the RealSense D415 (stereo IR) achieved ~2 mm error indoors and could capture vine canopy in full sun, whereas ToF and structured‐light sensors had larger bias. This work highlights the performance and limitations of stereo-depth systems for vineyard mapping【2†L157-L165】.  

# Dense LiDAR Mapping  
LiDAR systems (terrestrial or mobile) are widely used to densely scan vines and orchards. Moreno *et al.* (2020) mount a 2D ToF LiDAR on a ground vehicle to scan trellised vines from multiple passes; the resulting high-resolution 3D point clouds (with points in every leafless branch) are fused via RTK‐GPS to form a complete vine model【35†L538-L547】. They show that these LiDAR‐based point clouds correlate strongly (R²≈0.85) with manually measured branch biomass. Likewise, Chakraborty *et al.* (2019) integrate a 3D LiDAR and IMU on a tractor to map fruit crops (including grapevines). Their system produces realistic canopy models – LiDAR‐derived canopy volume (convex-hull) correlates at r≈0.81 with manual volume measurements – and it can monitor seasonal vine growth【43†L88-L93】. Finally, Petrović *et al.* (2022) compare terrestrial LiDAR vs. UAV photogrammetry in vineyards. They find both methods reconstruct canopy geometry accurately (correlation ≈0.9 with ground-truth leaf counts)【39†L1-L4】. These studies illustrate that LiDAR yields very dense 3D models of vines, suitable for volume and biomass estimation.  

- **Moreno et al. (2020)** – *On-ground vineyard reconstruction using a LiDAR-based automated system (Sensors)*.  Uses a downward-facing 2D LiDAR on a mobile cart to repeatedly scan vine rows. The scans (with millimeter precision) are georeferenced via RTK-GNSS and fused into a high-density 3D cloud. An α-shape is fitted to the cloud to compute branch volumes; LiDAR-derived volumes show strong linear correlation with actual biomass【35†L538-L547】.  
- **Chakraborty et al. (2019)** – *Mobile 3D LiDAR canopy mapping for tree fruits (Computers and Electronics in Agriculture)*.  Describes a ROS-based system combining 3D LiDAR and IMU on a ground vehicle. They develop voxel-grid and convex-hull methods for extracting canopy height and volume from the LiDAR point clouds. Field tests show LiDAR volume estimates strongly match manual measurements (e.g. convex-hull volume vs. apple canopy volume: r=0.81)【43†L88-L93】.  
- **Petrović et al. (2022)** – *Vine canopy reconstruction with terrestrial LiDAR and aerial imaging (Remote Sensing)*.  Compares static/terrestrial LiDAR scans with UAV photogrammetry on defoliated vines. Both sensors produce high-fidelity canopy models – regression with ground-truth leaf count yields R≈0.9 for each method【39†L1-L4】. This indicates that LiDAR (and multi-view photogrammetry) can faithfully capture vine geometry.  

# Point-Cloud Registration (ICP and Related Algorithms)  
Accurate alignment of point clouds is critical. Si *et al.* (2022) provide an extensive survey of registration methods, covering classical ICP variants, probabilistic approaches (e.g. NDT), feature‐matching methods (e.g. RANSAC on FPFH/SHOT features), and emerging deep‐learning models【45†L483-L492】. Zhang *et al.* (2020) review deep-learning–based registration: they classify methods into correspondence-based and correspondence-free (end-to-end) approaches, detailing modules such as feature extractors and outlier rejection【51†L54-L62】. On algorithms, Mellado *et al.* (2014) introduce *Super4PCS*, a fast global-registration algorithm that matches four-point bases and achieves linear-time complexity. Super4PCS can align raw point clouds in arbitrary poses (even with ~25% overlap) far faster than earlier 4PCS【53†L47-L56】. In addition, techniques like *Go-ICP* (Yang et al. 2015) use branch-and-bound to guarantee global optimality of ICP, and learned methods like *LORAX* (Elbaz et al. 2017) use deep auto-encoders on local “superpoints” to compute descriptors for coarse alignment followed by fine ICP refinement【59†L15-L24】. In practice, ICP (Besl & McKay 1992) remains the workhorse, often augmented by pre-alignment (e.g. feature matching with FPFH and RANSAC) or iterative optimization (e.g. Anderson-accelerated ICP) to improve convergence in cluttered scenes【43†L88-L93】【45†L483-L492】. 

- **Si et al. (2022)** – *Survey of point cloud registration algorithms (Appl. Sci.)*. Comprehensive review of registration techniques (hierarchical, probabilistic, feature-based, deep learning) and their trade-offs【45†L483-L492】. They note that despite advances (including deep learning), robustness to outliers and generalization remain challenges in practice.  
- **Zhang et al. (2020)** – *Deep learning based point cloud registration: an overview (VR & Intelligent Hardware)*. Reviews recent neural approaches for registration. Methods are categorized (correspondence-based vs. correspondence-free) and key modules (feature extraction, matching, etc.) are discussed【51†L54-L62】.  
- **Mellado et al. (2014)** – *Super4PCS: Fast global registration via smart indexing (Computer Graphics Forum)*. Introduces Super4PCS, which removes the quadratic complexity of earlier 4PCS by efficient hashing. It performs optimal global alignment in linear time with respect to point count, enabling very fast outlier-tolerant registration of raw scans without ICP refinement【53†L47-L56】.  
- **Elbaz et al. (2017)** – *LORAX: 3D point cloud registration via deep auto-encoder (CVPR)*. Proposes a deep-learning approach for localizing a close-range scan within a large-scale map. LORAX selects overlapping “superpoints” in both scans, encodes their local geometry via a deep auto-encoder, and matches these descriptors to infer coarse correspondences, followed by fine alignment. This yields robust registration even with large initial misalignment【59†L15-L24】.  

# Multi-Sensor Integration and SfM Relocalization  
Several studies address aligning heterogeneous scans (e.g. depth-camera or LiDAR point clouds) to a pre-built SfM model or map. Wang *et al.* (2025) demonstrate a two-phase plant reconstruction: first they use high-resolution camera images in an SfM/MVS pipeline to produce accurate single-view vine point clouds, then they merge six overlapping stereo‐derived point clouds into a complete vine model. This is done via a coarse marker-based self-alignment followed by ICP refinement【56†L380-L388】, effectively “relocalizing” the stereo scans within the SfM-derived frame. Similarly, Akhihiero & Gross (2025) propose a relocalization framework that aligns a new scan to a prior map: they extract ISS keypoints and FPFH descriptors on both the target (new scan) and source (prior map) clouds, estimate an initial transform from descriptor matches, then apply a Normal Distributions Transform (NDT) for coarse fitting and ICP for final refinement【58†L28-L33】. These multi-stage pipelines (feature-matching → NDT → ICP) handle large initial offsets and noise. In general, aligning a camera/LiDAR scan to an SfM point cloud relies on the same registration tools: keypoint detection (e.g. ISS/FPFH or learned features), initial pose estimation (possibly RANSAC), and ICP-like fine tuning. For example, Elbaz *et al.*’s LORAX shows that deep descriptors can match a local scan to a large-scale model without any initial pose at all【59†L15-L24】. Such methods form the basis for “relocalization” within a vineyard map: one obtains a dense vine model (from structure-from-motion) in global coordinates, then uses point-cloud registration (ICP/NDT with features) to compute the rigid transform that aligns a new stereo/LiDAR scan to that map.  

- **Wang et al. (2025)** – *Accurate plant 3D reconstruction via stereo imaging and multi-view alignment (Front. Plant Sci.)*. Integrates SfM/MVS with multi-view stereo: high-res images first yield detailed single-view models; then six stereo-camera point clouds are aligned into one vine model using marker-based coarse registration and fine ICP【56†L380-L388】. This exemplifies combining SfM and depth scans for complete 3D vine models.  
- **Akhihiero & Gross (2025)** – *Pointcloud registration for relocalization (ArXiv)*. Presents a relocalization pipeline for robotic mapping. ISS keypoints and FPFH descriptors are matched between the new scan and the existing point-cloud map, an initial 3D transform is estimated, then a Normal Distributions Transform (NDT) is applied and finally ICP refines the alignment【58†L28-L33】. This multi-step strategy effectively aligns a proximity scan to a prior map even under noise or partial overlap.  
- **Elbaz et al. (2017)** – *LORAX (see above)*. Although framed for autonomous driving, LORAX’s approach of learning deep descriptors for overlapping “superpoints” provides a way to register a close-range point cloud to a large-scale map without manual initialization【59†L15-L24】. Such deep-learning registration could in principle be adapted to align a depth-camera scan of a vine into a high-fidelity SfM map.  

Each of these papers contributes relevant techniques for our scenario: the stereo-depth and LiDAR works show how to acquire detailed vine point clouds, the registration surveys and algorithms describe how to align any two clouds, and the multi-sensor studies demonstrate relocalizing a new scan within an existing 3D model using feature matching and ICP/NDT refinement.
