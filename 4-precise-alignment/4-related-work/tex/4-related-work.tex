\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:precise-alignment-related-work}

The precise localisation of a robotic platform within a vineyard environment spans three complementary technical domains. First, the robot must generate high-fidelity, metrically scaled three-dimensional representations of the immediate vine environment from its onboard sensors. Second, a global structural reference map must already exist against which those local observations can be compared; in the system described in this thesis, this reference is a Structure from Motion (SfM) point cloud extracted from a 3D Gaussian Splatting model, though LiDAR-derived maps serve the same purpose in other systems. Third, reliable algorithms must compute the rigid transformation that aligns the local capture to the global frame, recovering the robot's pose with centimetric accuracy. The following review surveys each domain in turn: proximal three-dimensional sensing via stereo, RGB-D, and LiDAR modalities; the construction of dense reference maps from LiDAR and photogrammetric pipelines; and the class of point cloud registration algorithms built upon the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) framework.

\subsection{Stereo Depth Imaging}
\label{subsec:stereo-depth}

Stereo and RGB-D imaging systems are widely used to generate proximal three-dimensional representations of individual plants. Unlike LiDAR, these systems are compact and inexpensive, making them practical for mounting on a field robotics platform. Depth is recovered by computing the disparity $d$ between a spatially separated pair of images, related to metric depth $Z$ by the pinhole model:
%
\begin{equation}
    Z = \frac{f \cdot B}{d}
\end{equation}
%
where $f$ is the focal length and $B$ is the baseline between the two image sensors. The principal challenge in vineyard settings arises from the self-occluding geometry of vine canopies and the variability of outdoor illumination.

Early work by Klodt et al.~\cite{klodtFieldPhenotypingGrapevine2015} established that passive stereo cameras are sufficient for quantitative vine characterisation. Their dense reconstruction pipeline generated depth maps from paired RGB images, enabling background subtraction and 3D estimation of leaf area and fruit-to-leaf ratios directly in the field, without requiring a controlled background or calibration rig. This demonstrated that the stereo approach can yield reliable vine structure measurements under realistic outdoor conditions. Parr et al.~\cite{parrAnalysisDepthCameras2022} subsequently benchmarked five depth sensor modalities on grape clusters under both laboratory and open-field conditions, comparing structured light, active-infrared (IR) stereo, time-of-flight, and LiDAR-based sensors. Their results showed that the RealSense D415 active IR stereo sensor achieved approximately 2\,mm error in controlled indoor conditions and remained functional in full sunlight, whereas time-of-flight and structured-light sensors exhibited larger measurement bias. These comparative findings provide an empirical basis for depth sensor selection in proximal vineyard tasks.

Modern acquisition pipelines have shifted towards offline Structure from Motion and Multi-View Stereo (SfM/MVS) processing of high-resolution images, rather than relying on the camera's integrated disparity engine. Wang et al.~\cite{wangAccuratePlant3D2025} proposed and validated a two-phase plant 3D reconstruction workflow in which SfM/MVS processing of images taken from six viewpoints generates high-fidelity, single-view point clouds free from the geometric distortion inherent to single-shot disparity computation. These partial clouds are then registered into a unified model via a coarse marker-based alignment followed by ICP fine refinement. Evaluated on two \textit{Ilex} species, the method achieved coefficients of determination ($R^2$) exceeding 0.92 for plant height and crown width, validating its suitability for plant-level quantitative phenotyping. A related formulation by Tamura et al.~\cite{tamuraUnsupervisedMonocularDepth2025} applied unsupervised deep learning to omnidirectional monocular imagery for 3D position estimation of grape berries in the wild. The model was trained using photometric reconstruction error and structural similarity (SSIM) as loss functions, enabling depth inference on the textureless surfaces typical of tightly packed grape clusters---surfaces on which conventional feature-matching stereo typically fails.

More recently, foundation model approaches have been applied to stereo depth estimation with the goal of achieving reliable zero-shot generalisation across varied imaging conditions. FoundationStereo~\cite{wenFoundationStereoZeroShot2025}
addresses the sim-to-real transfer problem by training on a curated dataset of one million photorealistic synthetic stereo pairs and employing a Side-Tuning Adapter (STA) to leverage monocular depth priors from DepthAnything V2; cost volumes are subsequently filtered via an Attentive Hybrid Cost Filtering (AHCF) module combining Axial-Planar Convolutions with a Disparity Transformer for long-range disparity reasoning. Evaluated on the Middlebury and ETH3D benchmarks, the model achieves state-of-the-art results and generalises well to in-the-wild data, as illustrated in \cref{fig:zeroShotFS}. In the present work, FoundationStereo is applied to imagery from a ZED Mini stereo camera to produce the depth maps from which the source point cloud is derived prior to registration.

Active RGB-D sensors, including the Intel RealSense series and the Microsoft Azure Kinect, use infrared projection to supply artificial texture in scenes that would be feature-poor under passive stereo alone. Miranda et al.~\cite{mirandaAssessingAutomaticData2023} evaluated the Azure Kinect for in-field fruit size estimation and documented a critical limitation: direct sunlight saturates the IR projector, leading to systematic depth failures in open canopy conditions. This sensitivity to ambient illumination is a primary practical constraint for any deployment relying on active structured-light depth in outdoor vineyards.

Multi-view registration is a necessary extension when reconstructing structurally complex plants that cannot be captured adequately from a single viewpoint. Stumpe et al.~\cite{stumpe3DMultimodalImage2025} addressed this challenge in the context of plant phenotyping, proposing a multimodal 3D image registration method that integrates time-of-flight depth data to mitigate the parallax and occlusion artefacts that arise when fusing images from different viewpoints and sensor types.

\subsection{Dense LiDAR Mapping}
\label{subsec:dense-lidar-mapping}

The generation of a dense, georeferenced global map is a prerequisite for the relocalisation paradigm. Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR)---an active, illumination-invariant modality that measures precise time-of-flight returns---has become the primary technology for constructing such reference maps in outdoor agricultural environments \cite{farhanComprehensiveReviewLiDAR2024}. Unlike stereo imaging, LiDAR is unaffected by scene texture and performs consistently during the dormant season when vine architecture is reduced to bare wood, which is precisely when precision pruning operations are carried out.

Llorens et al.~\cite{llorensGeoreferencedLiDAR3D2011} demonstrated georeferenced tractor-mounted vineyard mapping by combining a SICK LiDAR profiler with a GPS receiver. By establishing the spatial correspondence between canopy return density and GPS coordinates at driving speed, they produced vineyard-scale canopy maps that were overlaid onto satellite imagery. This side-scanning acquisition paradigm---in which the sensor traverses the row and records multiple returns per vine---established the operational approach adopted by most subsequent ground-level systems. Moreno et al.~\cite{morenoOnGroundVineyardReconstruction2020} extended the concept with a downward-facing 2D time-of-flight LiDAR mounted on an electric platform georeferenced via RTK-GNSS, generating high-density 3D point clouds of dormant vine rows. Applying $\alpha$-shape fitting to extract branch volumes, they reported a linear correlation with actual dry biomass of $R^2 = 0.85$, confirming that LiDAR volumetrics provide a reliable proxy for agronomically relevant pruning wood measurements. A comparable mobile 3D LiDAR and IMU system was evaluated by Chakraborty et al.~\cite{chakrabortyEvaluationMobile3D2019} for canopy mapping in fruit crops including grapevines; voxel-grid and convex-hull volume methods yielded a Pearson correlation of $r \approx 0.81$ with manual measurements, with the added capability of monitoring seasonal canopy development over multiple visits.

The integration of SLAM with LiDAR enables simultaneous pose tracking and map construction during robot traversal, eliminating the dependence on external GNSS in canopy-occluded or signal-denied environments. Tagarakis et al.~\cite{tagarakisDigitalMappingPruning2026} demonstrated SLAM-based LiDAR for generating digital maps of pruning wood biomass across vineyard parcels, illustrating how a pre-built structural map can directly support precision viticulture decision-making. Combining LiDAR structural data with complementary sensing modalities improves the semantic richness of the resulting maps. Pavol Jozef et al.~\cite{pavoljozefsafarikuniversityinkosiceSynergisticUseLiDAR2025} fused UAV hyperspectral imagery with LiDAR-derived canopy height models in the Tokaj vineyard region and found that spectral classification alone achieved only 48.9\% producer accuracy for vine detection, whereas adding structural LiDAR features improved overall accuracy to 96\%. The AgriLiRa4D benchmark dataset~\cite{zhanAgriLiRa4DMultiSensorUAV2026} extends multi-modal agricultural mapping to the aerial domain, providing synchronised 3D LiDAR, 4D radar, and IMU data from UAV flights over flat, hilly, and terraced farmland types. The dataset is specifically constructed to evaluate multi-sensor SLAM algorithms under the challenges characteristic of agricultural fields---including low-texture crops, repetitive geometric patterns, and dynamic vegetation---and defines a public benchmark for comparative evaluation.

Beyond its established role in large-scale mapping, LiDAR is equally applicable as a proximal sensing modality for capturing individual vine geometry at close range. When mounted on a robotic platform that halts adjacent to a target vine, a spinning 3D LiDAR sensor accumulates dense returns from the vine structure over a short acquisition period. The resulting point cloud, though captured from a limited set of viewpoints, can provide millimetre-resolution coverage of the exposed wood and cane geometry---a capability that is particularly valuable during the dormant season, when visual texture is sparse and passive stereo matching becomes unreliable. This proximal use of LiDAR forms one of the two sensing modalities evaluated in the present work.

A direct comparison of LiDAR and photogrammetric reconstruction on defoliated vines was conducted by Petrović et al.~\cite{petrovicVineCanopyReconstruction2022}, who found that both terrestrial LiDAR and UAV photogrammetry reconstruct canopy geometry with comparable fidelity, each achieving regression against ground-truth geometry of $R \approx 0.9$. This equivalence indicates that dense SfM-generated point clouds are a viable alternative to direct LiDAR for constructing global reference maps, provided sufficient image overlap and resolution are maintained during acquisition. In the system described in this thesis, the global reference is an SfM-derived point cloud extracted from a 3D Gaussian Splatting model of each vine, and it is against this reference that the locally acquired source cloud is registered.

Whether the global reference originates from LiDAR or from SfM photogrammetry, and whether the local observation is captured by a stereo camera or a spinning LiDAR, the final alignment step requires a common algorithmic framework for registering the two point clouds.

\subsection{Point Cloud Registration}
\label{subsec:point-cloud-registration}

Point cloud registration is the core computational operation in the precise alignment problem: given a local point cloud captured by the robot's onboard sensor and a pre-built global reference map, the objective is to find the rigid transformation---comprising a rotation matrix $\mathbf{R} \in SO(3)$ and a translation vector $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^3$---that minimises the geometric discrepancy between the two sets. The canonical formulation, proposed independently by Besl and McKay~\cite{beslMethodRegistration3D1992a} and by Chen and Medioni~\cite{chenObjectModelingRegistration1991}, is the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm. Given a source point set $\mathbf{P}$ and a target set $\mathbf{Q}$, ICP minimises:
%
\begin{equation}
    E(\mathbf{R}, \mathbf{t}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \| \mathbf{R} \cdot \mathbf{p}_i + \mathbf{t} - \mathbf{q}_i \|^2
    \label{eq:icp}
\end{equation}
%
by alternating between a nearest-neighbour correspondence search and a closed-form transformation update via singular value decomposition, repeating until convergence. Rusinkiewicz and Levoy~\cite{rusinkiewiczEfficientVariantsICP2001} provided an influential taxonomy of ICP design choices, demonstrating that the choice of point selection strategy, correspondence metric, and minimisation kernel each independently determine convergence rate and final accuracy. Huang et al.~\cite{huangComprehensiveSurveyPoint2021} synthesise the connections between classical optimisation-based approaches and the emerging class of learned registration methods in a comprehensive survey.

Several extensions of ICP improve robustness in the geometrically repetitive, texturally ambiguous structure of vineyard rows. Segal et al.~\cite{segalGeneralizedICP2009} introduced Generalised ICP, which reformulates the alignment objective as a probabilistic plane-to-plane metric, modelling local surface patches as Gaussian distributions. This formulation is more robust to incorrect correspondences than the standard point-to-point variant and requires less manual tuning of the maximum correspondence distance threshold. He et al.~\cite{heIterativeClosestPoints2017} proposed GF-ICP, a feature-augmented variant that incorporates point curvature, surface normals, and local density into the correspondence cost. This enriched error metric enables convergence from larger initial errors than pure geometric proximity allows. Choi et al.~\cite{choiIterativeKClosestPoint2020} extended ICP to coloured point clouds through a probabilistic K-closest matching formulation with a colour-supported objective, adding a photometric constraint that reduces correspondence ambiguity in regions with visually distinct surface texture---a useful property when aligning vine bark or cane surface reconstructions.

Semantic ICP, as developed by Chen et al.~\cite{chenSemanticICPIterativeClosest2025}, restricts correspondence matching to points sharing the same semantic class label. This prevents the structurally plausible but semantically incorrect matches that arise when multiple anatomical regions are present in a single scan, improving robustness in multi-organ plant reconstructions. Vizzo et al.~\cite{vizzoKISSICPDefensePointtoPoint2023} demonstrated with KISS-ICP that a deliberately lean point-to-point formulation, augmented by adaptive correspondence thresholding and a robust kernel for outlier rejection, achieves competitive LiDAR odometry accuracy across diverse sensor platforms and environments without requiring per-deployment parameter tuning. Their finding that engineering simplicity can match or exceed more complex variants in practice has informed the design of several subsequent registration systems.

A fundamental limitation common to all ICP variants is sensitivity to the initial pose estimate: convergence to the global optimum of \cref{eq:icp} is not assured when the initial alignment error exceeds the geometric scale of distinctive scene features. For the more challenging case of global registration from an arbitrary initial pose, Mellado et al.~\cite{melladoSuper4PCSFastGlobal2014} introduced Super4PCS, which detects congruent four-point bases across two overlapping point clouds in linear time, enabling alignment in arbitrary initial orientations with as little as 25\% overlap. Yang et al.~\cite{yangGoICPGloballyOptimal2016} proposed Go-ICP, which employs a branch-and-bound search over the $SE(3)$ space of rigid transformations, guaranteeing globally optimal ICP solutions by systematically excluding sub-optimal rotation regions at the cost of increased computation.

The most recent direction adapts deep learning to replace hand-crafted descriptors with learned representations. Elbaz et al.~\cite{elbaz3DPointCloudRegistration2017} trained a deep auto-encoder to encode overlapping local regions of a point cloud into compact feature vectors; matched descriptor pairs initialise a subsequent ICP refinement, enabling robust alignment even under the large initial misalignment typical when registering a close-range scan against a large-scale prior map. Zhang et al.~\cite{zhangDeepLearningBased2020} provide a systematic review of deep learning-based registration methods, concluding that learned descriptors consistently outperform hand-crafted alternatives on standard benchmarks, in particular under noise and low-overlap conditions. Xu et al.~\cite{xuCross3DRegLargescaleRealworld2025} address the further challenge of cross-source registration---aligning point clouds from heterogeneous sensor types, such as a spinning mechanical LiDAR and a semi-solid-state LiDAR---through a visual-geometric attention module that fuses image and geometric features to establish robust correspondences across point clouds with different density and pattern distributions. A global survey by Yin et al.~\cite{yinSurveyGlobalLiDAR2024} identifies the central open challenge in LiDAR-based global localisation as reliably retrieving the correct initial alignment hypothesis within a large-scale map, noting that the dominant operational paradigm---global place retrieval followed by local ICP refinement---remains fragile under seasonal appearance change, a challenge directly relevant to long-term autonomous deployment in vineyards.
